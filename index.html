Probability, Cause–Effect, and a Godless Universe

By Karthik Hegde

Introduction

Many people explain life using words like God, karma, destiny, or luck. These ideas sound comforting, but they are not necessary to understand how the universe actually works. A simpler and more consistent explanation exists:

The universe operates through cause–effect relationships and probability, not moral justice.

This article argues that reality is governed by probability and cause–effect relationships, not karma or moral justice. It explains how events arise from prior conditions interacting with randomness, rather than from rewards or punishments. Based on this view, the article concludes that rational living means engineering better odds by taking actions that measurably increase the probability of outcomes you want.

Cause–Effect: A Neutral Law

Cause–effect is a basic principle of reality:

Every event happens because certain conditions existed before it.

This principle is neutral. It has:

  • No morality
  • No intention
  • No judgment

Simple example

My friend came to my room. Because of that, a conversation happened.

The universe did not decide this conversation should happen. There was no good or bad involved. The event simply followed from prior conditions.

Cause–effect explains why something happened — not whether it should have happened.

Karma: A Moral Belief, Not a Law

Karma claims something very different:

  • If you do good things, good things will happen to you.
  • If you do bad things, bad things will happen to you.

This assumes the universe has:

  • A moral memory
  • A sense of justice
  • A system of delayed reward and punishment

There is no scientific evidence for such a system.

My view about Karma

Humans dislike uncertainty and unfairness. In early societies, the idea of karma likely served a social purpose: encouraging people to behave well by promising future rewards and discouraging wrongdoing by threatening punishment.

However, reality often contradicted this belief. People observed clear exceptions — individuals who did bad things but still succeeded, and good people who suffered. Instead of rejecting karma, new explanations were introduced, such as past lives or hidden deeds, to protect the belief.

This pattern shows that karma evolved by adding assumptions whenever exceptions appeared, rather than being tested and revised like a scientific idea. Over time, these explanations became flexible enough to justify any outcome, making karma unfalsifiable.

Cause–effect does not require such adjustments. It accepts exceptions, randomness, and unfair outcomes without inventing new stories. It does not promise justice — it promises consistency.

Why Cause–Effect Is Not Karma

The difference is critical:

  • Cause–effect is descriptive (what happened and why)
  • Karma is prescriptive (what should happen)

Cause–effect allows:

  • Good things to happen to bad people
  • Bad things to happen to good people
  • Random accidents
  • Unfair outcomes

Karma does not. Reality matches cause–effect, not karma.

Probability: The Role of Uncertainty

Not all causes guarantee outcomes. Many only change probability.

Example: an interview

  • Studying increases the probability of success
  • Not studying decreases it
  • Neither guarantees the result

Effort does not ensure success — it only shifts the odds.

This is how:

  • Exams work
  • Trading works
  • Scientific experiments work
  • Life works

Luck and Randomness

What people call luck is simply:

Outcomes influenced by variables outside our control

Randomness does not mean nothing matters. Instead, it means:

  • You cannot control everything
  • But you can control some causes

Rational action focuses only on controllable variables.

A Rational Life Strategy

If the universe is probabilistic and morally neutral, then the most logical way to live is:

Do actions that increase the probability of the outcome you want.

In simple terms, if there is no God controlling outcomes and no karma guaranteeing fairness, then success becomes a numbers game. You do not ask what should happen — you ask what increases my chances.

For example, consider an interview happening today. What increases the probability of clearing it?

  • Reading and revising today increases the probability
  • Not preparing keeps the probability low

Studying does not guarantee success, but it clearly improves the odds. Prayer, hope, or worrying do not change the probability in any measurable way. Rational action means doing the thing that mathematically improves your chances, and ignoring everything else.

This means:

  • Preparation instead of prayer
  • Skill instead of hope
  • Adjustment instead of expectation

You do not wait for fairness. You engineer better odds.

Why People Prefer Karma

Humans dislike uncertainty. Karma provides:

  • Emotional comfort
  • A sense of fairness
  • Meaning in suffering

But comfort is not truth.

Cause–effect does not promise justice — it promises consistency.

Conclusion

The universe is not good or bad. It is morally neutral and probabilistic.

Events are not rewards or punishments. They are consequences of prior conditions interacting with randomness.

Understanding this removes false hope — but it gives something stronger:

Control over your actions.

You may not control outcomes, but you can definitely influence the odds of those outcomes.

Instead of gaining confidence by thinking "I have prayed, so things will work out", a more practical confidence comes from this thought: "I have done everything that increases the probability of success. What remains is randomness, but the odds are now in my favor."
